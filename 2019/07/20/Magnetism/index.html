Electric Flux and Gauss’s Law

The concept of electric flux

Today’s whole new concept is Electric Flux. Suppose we have a electric field and we bring in that the electric field a surface, an open surface, and I carve this surface up in very small elements, each with size $dA$, a very teeny weeny area.


Then we take a direction as the normal $\hat{n}$. And at that location, the local electric field may be $\vec{E}$. Hence the electric flux $d\phi$ go through the little surface $dA$ is defined as the dot product of $E$ and the vector perpendicular to this element.

$d\phi=\vec{E}\cdot\hat{n}\cdot dA=\vec{E}\cdot \vec{dA}=E\cdot dA\cdot cos(\theta)$

$\vec{dA}$ is a vector whose magnitude equals the area of the little surface and its direction is equivalent to the unit normal vector $\hat{n}$. And $\theta$ is the angle between those two vectors.

Apparently, $d\phi$ is a scalar. The number can be larger, smaller than zero, and it can be zero. The SI unit of flux follows immediately from the definition $N\cdot C^{-1}\cdot m^2$.

And in this case you can calculate the flux through the entire surface by doing an integral.

$\phi=\int\vec{E}\cdot \vec{dA}$

Now we take a surface which is not open as the one we’ve talked above. It’s a completely closed surface like a potato or balloon.

Here we notice by convention the normal to the surface, locally to the surface, is always from the inside of the surface to the outside world.

It’s uniquely determined because it’s a close surface. For the open surface, which is not uniquely determined, we can abitrarily chose one but we could have flipped it over a hundred eighty degrees.

And now we can calculate the total flux through the closed surface by doing a integral over the closed surface.

$\phi =\oint \vec{E}\cdot \vec{dA}$

Also the flux $\phi$ is a scaler. It can be zero, positive or negative. If you think it as air, also flows out. If more flows out than flows in, then it’s positive. If more flows in than flows out, it’s negative.

Now let’s calculate the flux for a small case where I have a point charge $Q_+$. And I take a sophere as its closed surface, which has radius $R$. Pick out a small element $\vec{dA}$, which is radially outward. Electric field $\vec{E}$ at that point is also radial.

Since it’s sphere, the $\vec{E}$ in magnitude is everywhere the same, the distance is the same and the $\vec{dA}$ and $\vec{E}$ are parallel. The total flux through the spherical closed surface is simply the area of the surface of that sphere times $E_R$.

$\phi =4\pi R^2E_R=4\pi R^2\frac{Q_+}{4\pi R^2\epsilon_0}=\frac{Q_+}{\epsilon_0}$

It’s independent of $R$, the size of the sphere.

The flux is given by the charge which is at the center divided by $\epsilon_0$.

Now if we had chosen some other shape, not a sphere, but I have dented it, it clear that would be the same like the air flows out as the case in sphere.

The Gauss’s Law

The Gauss’s Law tells us that the electric flux going through the closed surface is sum of all charges inside the surface, that you may choose at any time you pick that surface, divided by $\epsilon_0$.

$$\phi = \oint\vec{E}\cdot\vec{dA}=\frac{\sum{Q_{ins}}}{\epsilon_{0}} $$

This is the first of four equations of Maxwell.

So electric flux through any closed surface is always the charge inside that closed surface divided by $\epsilon_0$. And if that flux happens to be zero, it means there is not net charge inside the closed surface. Gauss’s Law always holds no matter how wired the charge distribution inside that closed surface.

But Gauss’s Law won’t help too much if you don’t have a situation whereby the charges are distributed in a very symmetric way. Gauss’s Law holds but it doesn’t do any good if you want to calculate the electric field.

So the forms of symmetry will help a lot when you want to calculate the electric field.

Example one

Spherical symmetry

Spherical ball

Now if we go to the spherical which is inside the shell and we know that $E$ is radially outwards apart from a plus or minus sign, apart from the fact that the angle between $\vec{dA}$ and $\vec{E}$, could either be zero degrees or one hundred eighty degrees.

We know that the surface area times electric field is $4\pi r^2E=\frac{Q_{ins}}{\epsilon_0}$. There is no $Q_{ins}$ in this case, so the $E$ must be zero! It’s a nontrival and amazing result!

Because it means anywhere as it is still in the shell no matter what radius you choose, the electric field equals zero. Notice that there is another simliar situation in thin shell which gravity is zero no matter any place you choose as long as it is inside the shell.

In this case the symmetry arguments take into account that the charge is uniformly distributed. If the charge on the sphere had not been uniformaly distributed, we could not use the symmetry arguments, and therefore the electric field inside would in fact not have been zero.

Now we go larger $r$ which is outside of the shell, we take the closed spherical surface and according to the equation provided by Gauss’s Law, we have $E=\frac{Q_+}{4\pi r^2\epsilon_0}$.

We’ve seen this form before. If we had put all the charges right at the middle at the center, we would’ve gotten exactly the same answer. In other words, whether the charge is uniformly distriburted over a sphere, or whether the charge is , all of it, exactly right at the center of the sphere, that make no difference for $E$ as long as you’re outside the sphere.

Gauss’s Law and Coulomb’s Law in a way are the same law, they both linked the electric field with the charge. The key is the electric force fall of as one over $r^2$. If it doesn’t fall off as one over $r^2$, then Gauss’s Law would not even hold. And the electric field inside the uniformly charged thin shell would not be zero. SO it’s the immediate consequence of the fact that electric forces fall off as one over $r^2$.

cylindrical symmetry

A flat horizontal plane uniformly charged distributions

If we now pick a clever Gauss surface, the answer pops out very quickly that how does the electric field distribute. The answer is $\frac{Q_+}{2\epsilon_0}$

And this result is relatively to the linear size of that surface, if the flat is not infinitely large, then the $E$ would not go straight out of flat plane. You only have two contribution from those two planes, top and bottom.
